    Define the self-modeling lattice and prove that self-modeling locality maps onto Hamiltonian locality.

    **Part A: Lattice Definition**

    1. Define the lattice graph G = (V, E) where V is a set of sites (each site is a self-modeling system) and E encodes which pairs share a B-M boundary. State that G is input (topology not derived -- honest framing per Pitfall P2 in RESEARCH.md). State results for general G; note that examples will be computed for G = Z^1 and Z^2.

    2. Assign local algebras: A_x = M_n(C) at each site x in V. The self-adjoint part A_x^sa = M_n(C)^sa is the EJA from Paper 5. Cite Paper 5 for the result that self-modeling forces M_n(C)^sa (type exclusion). For a finite region R subset V, A_R = bigotimes_{x in R} A_x.

    3. Define the quasi-local algebra A = norm-closure of union_{R finite} A_R, following Bratteli-Robinson vol. 2, Ch. 6.2. Note this is a UHF algebra (uniformly hyperfinite) when all sites have the same local dimension n.

    4. Define the interaction as a map Phi from finite subsets of V to local observables: Phi({x,y}) = h_{xy} in A_{xy}^sa for {x,y} in E, and Phi(X) = 0 for |X| != 2 or X not an edge. This is the nearest-neighbor interaction structure.

    5. The Hamiltonian for a finite region Lambda: H_Lambda = sum_{{x,y} in E, {x,y} subset Lambda} h_{xy}.

    **Part B: Locality Mapping**

    Prove the following chain:

    1. State self-modeling locality precisely: In the self-modeling framework, the model M at site x can probe the body B at site x only through their shared boundary. The model cannot access the bulk of B directly. Across sites: site x's model can interact with site y's body only if x and y share a B-M boundary (i.e., {x,y} in E).

    2. State Hamiltonian locality precisely: The interaction Phi has support only on edges of G. That is, Phi(X) = 0 unless X = {x,y} for some edge {x,y} in E.

    3. Prove the mapping: The self-modeling constraint implies that any coupling between sites x and y must be mediated by their shared B-M boundary. If {x,y} is not an edge (no shared boundary), there is no channel through which x can influence y at the algebraic level. Therefore the interaction term Phi({x,y}) must vanish for non-edges. Conversely, for edges {x,y} in E, the shared B-M boundary provides a channel for coupling, and Phi({x,y}) = h_{xy} may be nonzero.

    4. Make the argument rigorous by appealing to the composite OUS structure (C1-C4 from Paper 5): the non-signaling axiom (C4) ensures that the only coupling between sites goes through the tensor product structure. For non-adjacent sites, there is no direct tensor factor connection, so the coupling vanishes. For adjacent sites, the product-form SP (a tensor b) & (c tensor d) = (a & c) tensor (b & d) provides the coupling structure.

    5. State explicitly: "Self-modeling locality (model probes body through boundary, not bulk) maps onto Hamiltonian locality (interaction supported on edges) because the composite OUS non-signaling constraint (C4) forbids direct coupling between non-adjacent sites, and the product-form SP provides the only algebraic channel for adjacent-site coupling."

    **Part C: Background Dependence Acknowledgment**

    State explicitly: "The lattice graph G = (V, E) is an input to this construction, not an output. The claim of the v3.0 program is that self-modeling determines the metric (encoded in the Hamiltonian coupling strengths and the resulting entanglement structure) on a given topology, not the topology itself. This is analogous to how GR determines the metric on a given manifold topology."

    Use conventions: natural units, (-,+,+,+) metric, a & b notation for SP, (a tensor b) & (c tensor d) = (a & c) tensor (b & d) for composite SP.
  </action>

  <name>Task 2: Construct h_{xy} from composite SP constraints and characterize the compatible family</name>
  <files>derivations/08-hamiltonian-construction.md, code/self_modeling_hamiltonian.py</files>
  <action>
    Derive the interaction Hamiltonian h_{xy} from the Paper 5 composite sequential product, count parameters, and characterize the family of compatible Hamiltonians.

    **Part A: Constraint Derivation (general n)**

    1. The two-site algebra is A_{xy} = M_n(C) tensor M_n(C) = M_{n^2}(C). A general self-adjoint element h_{xy} in A_{xy}^sa has n^4 real parameters.

    2. The self-modeling constraint requires that the two-site dynamics generated by h_{xy} reproduce the product-form SP at the algebraic level. Specifically: the composite SP (a tensor b) & (c tensor d) = (a & c) tensor (b & d) must be recovered from the Heisenberg evolution generated by h_{xy}.

    3. The single-site SP in the M_n(C)^sa representation is the Luders product: a & b = a^{1/2} b a^{1/2} (where a is an effect, 0 <= a <= 1). This is the concrete form after Paper 5's type exclusion.

    4. Translate the SP constraint into a Hamiltonian constraint. The key insight: the sequential product a & b describes the post-measurement state when effect a is measured and outcome "yes" occurs, followed by measurement of b. In the Hamiltonian framework, this corresponds to a specific unitary coupling. The constraint is:

       For all effects a, c in A_x^sa and b, d in A_y^sa:
       The Heisenberg-picture evolution of (a tensor b) under exp(i h_{xy} t) at leading order in t must be consistent with the product-form SP structure.

       Expand: exp(i h_{xy} t) (a tensor b) exp(-i h_{xy} t) = (a tensor b) + it [h_{xy}, a tensor b] + O(t^2).

       The SP constraint requires that the commutator [h_{xy}, a tensor b] has a specific tensor-product structure dictated by the Luders product on each factor.

    5. Write out the commutator constraint explicitly. For product operators a tensor b and c tensor d:
       [h_{xy}, a tensor b] must equal a term proportional to (a & c - c & a) tensor (b & d - d & b) or similar structure dictated by the SP.

       More precisely: the SP generates a "measurement back-action" where measuring a at site x affects b at site y through the coupling. The Hamiltonian h_{xy} must encode this back-action.

    6. Enumerate the constraint equations. Each pair of effects (a, b) gives constraints on h_{xy}. Count independent constraints vs. free parameters.

    **Part B: Explicit Construction for n = 2**

    1. For n = 2: A_x = M_2(C), A_{xy} = M_4(C). h_{xy} is a 4x4 self-adjoint matrix with 16 real parameters.

    2. The Luders product on M_2(C)^sa: for effects a = sum_i lambda_i p_i (spectral decomposition), a & b = a^{1/2} b a^{1/2}.

    3. Use the Pauli basis: any h in M_4(C)^sa can be written as
       h_{xy} = sum_{mu,nu=0}^{3} J_{mu nu} (sigma_mu tensor sigma_nu)
       where sigma_0 = I, sigma_{1,2,3} are Pauli matrices, and J_{mu nu} are real coefficients (16 parameters). Self-adjointness is automatic since Pauli matrices are self-adjoint.

    4. Apply the SP constraints to determine which J_{mu nu} are allowed. The product-form SP constraint (a tensor b) & (c tensor d) = (a & c) tensor (b & d) with Luders product a & c = a^{1/2} c a^{1/2} constrains the commutator structure.

    5. Specifically: the single-site terms J_{mu 0} (sigma_mu tensor I) and J_{0 nu} (I tensor sigma_nu) are unconstrained local terms (on-site energies). The coupling terms J_{mu nu} for mu, nu >= 1 encode the inter-site interaction. The SP structure constrains these 9 coupling parameters.

    6. Identify which coupling terms are compatible with the product-form SP. The key constraint: the Luders product a^{1/2} b a^{1/2} is symmetric under the exchange a <-> UaU^dagger for any unitary U that commutes with a. This SU(2) symmetry of the Luders product constrains h_{xy} to be SU(2)-covariant. Determine whether this forces J_{mu nu} = J delta_{mu nu} (isotropic, Heisenberg-like) or allows anisotropy.

    7. Report: (a) the number of free parameters remaining after SP constraints, (b) the explicit form of h_{xy} for n = 2, (c) whether the family is unique or parametric.

    **Part C: Numerical Verification (Python)**

    Write code/self_modeling_hamiltonian.py that:

    1. Constructs h_{xy} for n = 2 using the derived form.
    2. Verifies self-adjointness: ||h_{xy} - h_{xy}^dagger|| < 1e-14.
    3. Verifies SP reproduction: for random effects a, c in M_2(C)^sa (0 <= a, c <= I) and b, d in M_2(C)^sa (0 <= b, d <= I), compute:
       - The composite SP: (a tensor b) & (c tensor d) = (a & c) tensor (b & d) where a & c = a^{1/2} c a^{1/2}
       - The Heisenberg evolution: exp(i h_{xy} t) (c tensor d) exp(-i h_{xy} t) at small t
       - Verify that the leading-order commutator [h_{xy}, c tensor d] is consistent with the SP structure
    4. Verifies non-triviality: h_{xy} != 0 (the interaction exists).
    5. Counts free parameters by checking linear independence of constraints.

    Use numpy for matrix operations and sympy for symbolic constraint analysis.

    **Backtracking check:** If the SP constraints leave h_{xy} entirely unconstrained or force h_{xy} = 0, document this finding clearly. If the family is large (many free parameters), note this for Phase 9 robustness analysis.

    **CRITICAL: Do NOT write down h_{xy} = J (sigma dot sigma) by analogy with the Heisenberg model. The form must be DERIVED from the SP constraints. If the result happens to be Heisenberg-like, that is a derived conclusion, not an assumption.**
  </action>
