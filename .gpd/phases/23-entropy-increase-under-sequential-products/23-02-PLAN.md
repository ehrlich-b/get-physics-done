  <action>
  Using the explicit channel E from Plan 01, derive the entropy behavior under iteration.

  **Step 1: Import Plan 01 results.**
  Read 23-01-SUMMARY.md. Extract:
  - The explicit channel E for the SWAP dynamics (Interpretation B: unitary evolution + partial trace)
  - The unitality determination: is E(I/d) = I/d?
  - The entropy change formula Delta S for single step
  - The sign of Delta S for different initial state families

  **Step 2: Classify the channel.**
  Based on Plan 01's unitality result:

  Case A -- E is unital (doubly stochastic):
  - Lindblad 1975 theorem applies directly: S(E(rho)) >= S(rho) for all rho
  - Monotonic entropy increase under iteration follows immediately
  - State the theorem: "For any initial state rho_B, the sequence S(rho_B^(k)) is non-decreasing where rho_B^(k) = Tr_M[U^k(rho_B tensor rho_M)(U^dag)^k]"
  - Prove: the fixed point is the maximally mixed state I/d (by unitality)
  - Compute the convergence rate from the channel's spectral gap

  Case B -- E is non-unital:
  - Lindblad 1975 does NOT apply directly
  - Instead, analyze via:
    (a) The fixed point rho_* of E: find rho_* such that E(rho_*) = rho_*
    (b) The relative entropy: D(E(rho) || rho_*) <= D(rho || rho_*)
        (data processing inequality for relative entropy holds for ALL CPTP maps, not just unital)
    (c) Convert relative entropy monotonicity to von Neumann entropy statement:
        S(E(rho)) = S(rho_*) - D(E(rho) || rho_*) + correction terms
    (d) The von Neumann entropy increases IF D(rho || rho_*) decreases faster than the correction
    (e) Find conditions on rho for which this holds

  **Step 3: Iterated dynamics.**
  For the 2-qubit system with SWAP:
  - Compute rho_B^(k) for k = 1, 2, ..., N analytically (or identify the pattern)
  - The channel E depends on rho_M. After one step, rho_M changes too.
  - CRITICAL: For a 2-site system, after the SWAP evolution, rho_B and rho_M exchange information. The iteration is:
    rho_BM^(k+1) = U rho_BM^(k) U^dag
    rho_B^(k) = Tr_M[rho_BM^(k)]
  - This is just unitary evolution of the composite system. The reduced state rho_B(t) is periodic in t (Poincare recurrence for finite system).
  - Therefore: on a 2-site system, entropy oscillates -- it CANNOT increase monotonically (by periodicity).

  **Step 4: Multi-site lattice.**
  The monotonic increase requires a LATTICE (many sites), not just 2 sites:
  - On a lattice with N >> 1 sites, each site interacts with its neighbors via SWAP
  - The reduced state of a single site sees an effective bath from the rest
  - In the thermodynamic limit, the effective bath never recoheres -> entropy increase
  - This is precisely the content of Lindblad's repeated interaction framework
  - State the theorem for the lattice case, with the thermodynamic limit as the key ingredient
  - Cite Attal-Joye 2007 (repeated quantum interactions) if applicable

  **Step 5: Precise conditions (ENTR-02).**
  Identify when entropy increases vs decreases:
  - 2-site: entropy oscillates (periodic, no monotonic increase)
  - N-site finite: quasi-periodic, with effective relaxation time growing with N
  - Thermodynamic limit: monotonic relaxation to equilibrium
  - Conditions: "For the SWAP lattice with N sites, the reduced entropy of any subsystem A with |A| << N increases on timescales t << t_recurrence ~ exp(N), approaching the equilibrium value S_eq = ln(n) per site"

  **Step 6: Connection to Phase 26.**
  Assess implications:
  - Does the entropy increase result (however conditional) support the selection argument?
  - If entropy increases on relevant timescales (before Poincare recurrence), self-modelers experience an arrow of time
  - The key: Poincare recurrence time >> self-modeling timescale for large systems
  - This is the standard statistical mechanics argument for the Second Law

  **Step 7: Honest gap statement.**
  The entropy increase is a finite-system approximation (not exact for finite N). State this explicitly:
  - "Strictly, entropy oscillates for any finite system. The arrow of time emerges in the thermodynamic limit."
  - "For macroscopic self-modelers (N >> 1), the recurrence time is astronomically longer than any observation time."
  - This is NOT a weakness -- it IS the standard physics resolution.

  CITE Lindblad 1975 explicitly (either as the direct tool or as the tool that almost-applies with identified modification).
  CITE Paper 5 and Paper 6 for the Luders product and SWAP Hamiltonian definitions.
  Address the non-unital concern from the roadmap EXPLICITLY in a dedicated section.
