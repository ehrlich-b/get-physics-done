  <action>
    Derive the information-theoretic area law from the channel capacity of boundary bonds. This is the conceptually strongest argument because it connects directly to the information-theoretic nature of self-modeling locality.

    **Part A: Self-modeling locality as an information-flow constraint**

    Recall from Phase 8 (derivations/08-lattice-definition.md, Theorem 1): Self-modeling locality means that a model subsystem M can only learn about its body B through the shared boundary. In the lattice language, this translates to: information between site x and site y must flow through the bonds connecting them. There is no "action at a distance" -- all correlations are mediated by the local interaction h_xy along edges of the graph G.

    Formalize: For a bipartition V = A union B of the lattice, define the boundary as

    boundary(A) = {<x,y> in E : x in A, y in B}

    ALL information between A and B must flow through the |boundary(A)| bonds in this set. There is no other communication channel. This is a direct consequence of the locality of the interaction (nearest-neighbor, Phase 8) and the non-signaling property (C4 from Paper 5, verified in Phase 8).

    **Part B: Quantum channel capacity per bond**

    Each boundary bond connects two M_n(C) systems. Model each bond as a quantum channel from one n-dimensional Hilbert space to another. The quantum channel capacity of such a bond is bounded by:

    C_bond <= 2 * log(n) nats

    This is the maximum quantum mutual information that can be transmitted through a single use of a channel with input dimension n. The factor of 2 arises because quantum channels can transmit up to log(n) qubits, and each qubit carries up to 2 nats of mutual information (via superdense coding). More precisely, for a quantum channel acting on C^n, the one-shot quantum capacity is at most log(n), and the mutual information is at most 2*log(n) by the Holevo bound.

    For the self-modeling lattice with local dimension n (A_x = M_n(C)):
    - Each bond carries at most 2*log(n) nats of mutual information
    - For n=2 (qubits): C_bond <= 2*ln(2) = 1.386 nats

    **Part C: Data processing inequality and mutual information bound**

    The data processing inequality (DPI) states: if C is obtained from B by local processing, then I(A:C) <= I(A:B). Applied to our setting:

    All information that region A has about region B must have been communicated through the boundary bonds. By the DPI, the total mutual information between A and B cannot exceed the sum of the channel capacities of the boundary bonds:

    I(A:B) <= sum_{bonds in boundary(A)} C_bond <= 2*log(n) * |boundary(A)|

    Justification: Each boundary bond independently carries at most 2*log(n) nats. The total information flow is bounded by the sum (no entanglement between different boundary bonds can exceed the sum of individual capacities because the bonds act on distinct pairs of sites).

    Note: This step requires care. The DPI applies to sequential processing, not directly to spatial cuts. The precise statement is: for a bipartite pure state rho_{AB}, the mutual information I(A:B) is bounded by the entanglement across the cut, which in turn is bounded by the total entanglement capacity of the bonds crossing the cut. This follows from the structure of the Hamiltonian: the entanglement between A and B is generated solely by the boundary terms h_{xy} with x in A, y in B, and each such term can generate at most 2*log(n) nats of entanglement.

    Cite: Bravyi-Hastings-Verstraete 2006 (arXiv:quant-ph/0603121) for the result that entanglement generation rate across a cut is bounded by c*|boundary|. The channel capacity argument extends this to a static bound on equilibrium entanglement for states that are ground states or low-energy states of local Hamiltonians.

    **Part D: From MI bound to entropy area law (PURE STATES ONLY)**

    For a PURE global state |psi> on the full lattice:

    S(A) = S(B) (Schmidt decomposition symmetry)
    S(AB) = 0 (pure state has zero entropy)
    I(A:B) = S(A) + S(B) - S(AB) = 2*S(A)

    Combining with the MI bound:

    2*S(A) = I(A:B) <= 2*log(n) * |boundary(A)|

    Therefore:

    **S(A) <= log(n) * |boundary(A)|** (Eq. 09-02.1)

    This is an AREA LAW: the entanglement entropy of region A is bounded by the number of boundary bonds times log(n).

    For n=2 (qubits): S(A) <= ln(2) * |boundary(A)| = 0.693 * |boundary(A)| nats

    **Part E: Assumptions and gap statement**

    State precisely:

    Assumption A2 (Pure Global State): The physically relevant state of the self-modeling lattice is a pure state |psi>.

    This assumption is needed because: for mixed states rho_{AB}, I(A:B) = S(A) + S(B) - S(AB) and S(AB) > 0, so I(A:B) < 2*S(A). The MI area law does NOT imply an entropy area law for mixed states.

    When A2 holds: The channel capacity argument gives a rigorous area law.
    When A2 fails: Fall back to the WVCH thermal MI area law (Plan 01), which bounds I(A:B) but not S(A) directly.

    Physical motivation for A2: The global state of the universe is often assumed to be pure (unitarily evolving from a pure initial state). The self-modeling lattice as a closed system would have a pure global state. However, if the lattice is an open subsystem embedded in a larger system, the global state may be mixed.

    **Part F: Connection to self-modeling**

    The channel capacity argument is the MOST NATURAL route for self-modeling because:

    1. Self-modeling is fundamentally an information-theoretic constraint: the model can only contain information about the body that flows through the boundary.

    2. The boundary's finite dimension (M_n(C)) directly limits the information capacity to log(n) per bond.

    3. No reference to specific Hamiltonian dynamics, temperature, or spectral gap is needed -- the bound follows from the structure alone.

    4. The resulting area-law coefficient log(n) has a physical interpretation: each boundary bond can carry at most log(n) nats of entanglement, which is the maximum information a self-model can acquire about a neighboring site through a single bond.

    Both routes complement each other. Together they cover: pure states (channel capacity) and thermal states (WVCH). Plan 03 will synthesize.

    **Part H: Higher dimensions**

    For a hypercubic lattice Z^d, a cubic region A of linear size L has |boundary(A)| ~ 2d*L^{d-1}. The channel capacity bound gives:

    S(A) <= log(n) * 2d * L^{d-1}

    This is the correct area-law scaling S ~ L^{d-1} in d dimensions. The bound works in ALL dimensions without modification.
