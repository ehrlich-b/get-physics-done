    Synthesize Plans 01 and 02 into a coherent area-law argument that resolves the "which state?" problem and bridges to Jacobson's gravity derivation (Phase 10).

    **Part A: The "which state?" resolution**

    The self-modeling framework (Phases 4-5, Paper 5) specifies the LOCAL algebraic structure: each site has A_x = M_n(C), composites have product-form sequential product, interactions h_{xy} = JF (SWAP). This constrains the interaction but does NOT specify a unique global quantum state. Multiple global states are compatible with the local structure.

    Resolve by identifying three complementary perspectives on "which state has area-law entanglement":

    **Perspective 1 (Thermal states, any T > 0):** Under Assumption A1 (the physically relevant state is a thermal state rho_beta = exp(-beta*H)/Z at some finite T), the WVCH bound gives:

    I(A:B) <= 2*beta*|boundary(A)|*|J|

    This is a mutual information area law. It holds for BOTH signs of J and ALL spatial dimensions. The entropy S(A) itself has a volume-law thermal contribution, but the mutual information (which captures quantum correlations, not thermal noise) obeys area law.

    **Perspective 2 (Pure states):** Under Assumption A2 (the global state is pure), the channel capacity bound gives:

    S(A) <= log(n)*|boundary(A)|

    This is a von Neumann entropy area law. It requires pure global state but is otherwise unrestricted -- no gap, no specific Hamiltonian, no temperature.

    **Perspective 3 (Any state, via entanglement first law -- THE KEY INSIGHT):** Jacobson's 2016 argument does NOT need S(A) to obey area law. It needs DELTA S -- the change in entropy under small perturbations -- to scale with boundary area. The entanglement first law states:

    delta S(A) = delta <K_A>

    where K_A = -ln(rho_A) is the modular Hamiltonian of the reduced state rho_A = Tr_B(rho). This is an EXACT quantum information identity (first-order perturbation theory for von Neumann entropy), not an approximation.

    The key question is: does delta <K_A> scale with |boundary(A)|?

    Argue: For states of local Hamiltonians, the modular Hamiltonian K_A has its dominant support near the entangling surface (the boundary between A and B). This is known rigorously for:
    - Half-space of a CFT vacuum (Bisognano-Wichmann theorem: K = 2*pi * integral of stress-energy weighted by distance to boundary)
    - Rindler wedge in QFT (exact result: K is the boost generator)
    - Lattice systems with area-law entanglement (K is approximately a sum of boundary operators -- Peschel 2003, Eisler-Peschel 2009)

    For the self-modeling lattice: the interaction h_{xy} = JF is nearest-neighbor, so perturbations delta H that are local (supported on a region of size O(1) near the boundary) will produce delta <K_A> that scales with the boundary area, not the volume. This follows from the Lieb-Robinson bound: perturbations at distance d from the boundary affect K_A only through the light-cone propagation, which is exponentially suppressed beyond d ~ v_LR * t.

    Therefore: delta S(A) = delta <K_A> ~ |boundary(A)| for local perturbations.

    This is what Jacobson's argument needs. State this as:

    **Result (Phase 9 main result):** For the self-modeling lattice with H = sum JF_{xy}:
    - I(A:B) <= 2*beta*|boundary|*|J| (rigorous, Assumption A1: thermal state)
    - S(A) <= log(n)*|boundary| (rigorous, Assumption A2: pure state)
    - delta S(A) ~ |boundary| for local perturbations (physical argument, no additional assumption beyond locality of H -- which is established in Phase 8)

    **Part B: Jacobson bridge -- what Phase 10 needs**

    Jacobson's 2016 entanglement equilibrium argument requires:

    (J1) Entanglement entropy S(A) for a small geodesic ball A satisfies area law: S(A) ~ |boundary(A)| (or at least, delta S ~ |boundary| under perturbations). **STATUS: Established in Perspective 3 above.**

    (J2) The entanglement first law: delta S = delta <K> where K is the modular Hamiltonian. **STATUS: Exact QI identity -- always holds.**

    (J3) Maximal vacuum entanglement hypothesis (MVEH): S(A) is maximized for the vacuum state among all states with the same expectation value of T_{ab}. **STATUS: Not established for the self-modeling lattice. This is the main gap for Phase 10.**

    State: Phase 9 delivers (J1) -- the area-law entanglement structure. Phase 10 must handle (J2) and (J3). The main gap is (J3): MVEH.

    **Part C: Why ground-state analysis alone is insufficient**

    Summarize from Plan 01, Task 2:
    - FM (J<0): S = 0 -- trivially area law but zero entanglement is useless for Jacobson
    - AFM (J>0): S = (1/3)*ln(L) -- logarithmic violation of strict area law
    - Neither sign gives clean area law from ground-state analysis
    - Both signs are gapless: Hastings does NOT apply

    This is why Perspectives 1-3 are needed: they go beyond ground-state analysis to establish area-law structure through information-theoretic and thermodynamic routes that do not require a gap.

    **Part D: Sign-of-J ambiguity resolution for gravity**

    Address the sign ambiguity explicitly:

    1. The WVCH bound and channel capacity bound are sign-independent (depend on |J|).
    2. delta S ~ |boundary| for local perturbations is sign-independent (depends on locality of H, not sign of J).
    3. For the gravity connection: the physically relevant case is likely J > 0 (AFM) because:
       - FM ground state has zero entanglement, which cannot drive Jacobson's argument
       - AFM ground state has nontrivial entanglement (S ~ ln(L) in 1D, area law in D >= 2)
       - The thermal state at any finite T has nontrivial entanglement for both signs
    4. State: the area-law argument itself is sign-independent, but the gravity application likely requires J > 0 or finite T (for nontrivial entanglement). This is NOT an additional assumption -- it is a physical selection criterion.

    **Part H: Robustness analysis**

    1. Robustness under J perturbations: The WVCH bound depends on |J|; the channel capacity bound is J-independent. Both are continuous in J. Area law persists for any J != 0.

    2. Robustness under sign change: Both bounds are sign-independent. Area law holds for J > 0 and J < 0.

    3. Robustness under interaction perturbations: Phase 8 derived h_xy = JF as the UNIQUE self-modeling-compatible interaction (up to energy shift alpha*1). There is no family of perturbations within the self-modeling constraint -- the interaction form is rigid. If we consider perturbations h_xy = JF + epsilon*V that BREAK self-modeling: the WVCH bound still applies (it requires only locality and finite norm), so the MI area law persists. The channel capacity bound also persists (it depends only on local dimension, not interaction form).

    4. Robustness under topology change: Both bounds depend on |boundary(A)| for graph G. For any finite-coordination graph (bounded degree), the bounds give area law. For long-range interactions: WVCH still applies if interactions have finite norm, but the "boundary" concept changes. Channel capacity argument requires modification for non-local bonds.

    5. Robustness under local dimension change: Channel capacity bound scales as log(n) per bond -- valid for all n >= 2. WVCH bound is n-independent (depends only on ||h_xy|| = |J|).

    **Theorem (Area-Law Entanglement from Self-Modeling Locality):**

    Let G = (V, E) be a finite graph with local algebras A_x = M_n(C) and self-modeling interaction Hamiltonian H = sum_{<x,y>} JF_{xy}. Then:

    (a) [Thermal MI area law] For the Gibbs state rho_beta at any T > 0:
        I(A:B) <= 2*beta*|boundary(A)|*|J|

    (b) [Pure state S area law] For any pure state |psi> on the lattice:
        S(A) <= log(n)*|boundary(A)|

    (c) [delta S scaling] For local perturbations of H near a bipartition boundary, the change in entanglement entropy scales as:
        delta S(A) ~ O(|boundary(A)|)

    Part (a) follows from WVCH (2008), part (b) from quantum channel capacity bounds and DPI, part (c) from the entanglement first law and approximate locality of the modular Hamiltonian (physical argument).
