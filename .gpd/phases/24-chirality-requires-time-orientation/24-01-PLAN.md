  <action>
    Derive the precise statement and proof that Weyl spinor decomposition (chirality) requires time-orientation, working through these steps:

    **Step 1: Lorentzian Clifford algebra setup.**
    Define Cl(d-1,1) with generators Gamma_mu satisfying {Gamma_mu, Gamma_nu} = 2 eta_{mu nu} where eta = diag(+1,-1,...,-1). The volume form is:
      Gamma_* = i^k Gamma_0 Gamma_1 ... Gamma_{d-1}
    where k is chosen so that (Gamma_*)^2 = +1 in even dimensions. For d = 2m, k = m(m-1)/2 + m = m(m+1)/2 when using mostly-minus metric. Verify: for d=4, Gamma_5 = i Gamma_0 Gamma_1 Gamma_2 Gamma_3, and (Gamma_5)^2 = +1.

    **Step 2: Chirality projectors and Weyl decomposition.**
    The Weyl projectors are P_L = (1 + Gamma_*)/2 and P_R = (1 - Gamma_*)/2. The Dirac spinor decomposes as S = S_L + S_R where S_L = P_L S, S_R = P_R S. This decomposition is the definition of chirality.

    **Step 3: Time-reversal action on Gamma_*.**
    Under time reversal (or more precisely, reversal of time-orientation): the timelike basis vector e_0 -> -e_0, while spacelike vectors are unchanged. At the Clifford algebra level: Gamma_0 -> -Gamma_0, Gamma_i -> Gamma_i for i=1,...,d-1. Compute:
      Gamma_* -> i^k (-Gamma_0) Gamma_1 ... Gamma_{d-1} = -Gamma_*
    since Gamma_0 appears exactly once. Therefore P_L <-> P_R: flipping time-orientation exchanges left-handed and right-handed Weyl spinors.

    **Step 4: Why both orientations are needed.**
    The volume form Gamma_* depends on an ordered basis (hence orientation of the manifold = space-orientation), AND on which direction is timelike (time-orientation). Explicitly:
    - Flipping space-orientation (reversing one spatial gamma): Gamma_* -> -Gamma_* (one sign flip from one gamma)
    - Flipping time-orientation (reversing gamma_0): Gamma_* -> -Gamma_* (one sign flip from gamma_0)
    Therefore a CONSISTENT chirality (a globally defined Gamma_* with definite sign) requires BOTH space-orientation and time-orientation. This is the content of Lawson-Michelsohn Appendix D: Weyl spinor bundles exist only on time-oriented AND space-oriented Lorentzian manifolds.

    **Step 5: Connection to Paper 7's Euclidean Cl(6).**
    Paper 7 works with an INTERNAL Euclidean Cl(6) with volume form omega_6 = gamma_1 ... gamma_6. The connection to spacetime chirality works as follows:
    - In the full theory, the 10-dimensional Spin(10) representation lives on a Lorentzian manifold (from Paper 6's emergent spacetime).
    - The internal Cl(6) chirality omega_6 determines the decomposition 16 -> (4,2,1) + (4bar,1,2) under Pati-Salam.
    - The SPACETIME chirality (4-dimensional Gamma_5) determines which Weyl spinor of SO(3,1) each fermion transforms as.
    - The combined chirality (internal x spacetime) determines the full chiral structure of the SM: left-handed fermions are (4,2,1) under Pati-Salam AND left-handed under spacetime Lorentz group.
    - Time-orientation enters because the spacetime Gamma_5 involves Gamma_0 (timelike). Without time-orientation, there is no consistent spacetime chirality, and the internal chirality from omega_6 cannot be correlated with spacetime handedness.

    **Step 6: State the theorem precisely.**
    THEOREM (Chirality requires time-orientation): Let (M, g) be an even-dimensional Lorentzian manifold of signature (d-1,1). The spinor bundle S(M) admits a decomposition S = S_L + S_R into Weyl spinor bundles (defined by the Lorentzian volume form) if and only if M is both time-oriented and space-oriented. In the context of the h_3(O) program: the chiral SM fermion assignment determined by omega_6 (Paper 7, Thm 4.2) requires the emergent spacetime (Paper 6) to carry a time-orientation.

    Reference Lawson-Michelsohn explicitly: Appendix D discusses spinor representations of the Lorentz group. Chapter II, Sec. 1 covers spin structures on oriented manifolds. The key point is that in Lorentzian signature, the relevant group is Spin(d-1,1), and the Weyl decomposition requires a choice of volume form, which requires both orientations.

    **CRITICAL**: Address the forbidden proxy explicitly. The internal Cl(6) omega_6 is Euclidean and does NOT directly involve time. The chirality-time link enters because: (a) omega_6 defines L/R for the INTERNAL quantum numbers (which SM representation each state carries), but (b) the PHYSICAL chirality (which Lorentz representation each fermion has) requires spacetime Gamma_5, which involves Gamma_0. The full SM chiral structure requires BOTH internal and spacetime chirality to be defined, hence requires time-orientation.

  <name>Task 2: Verify lattice framing implies spin structure</name>

  <action>
    Establish the hierarchy: framing => spin structure => orientability, and verify that Paper 6's lattice provides the equivalent of a framing in the continuum limit.

    **Step 1: Definitions.**
    - FRAMING of an n-manifold M: a trivialization of the tangent bundle TM, i.e., n globally defined linearly independent vector fields {e_1, ..., e_n}. Equivalently, a section of the frame bundle F(M) -> M.
    - SPIN STRUCTURE on an oriented Riemannian n-manifold (M, g): a lift of the SO(n) frame bundle P_SO(M) to a Spin(n) principal bundle P_Spin(M), i.e., a double cover that is compatible with the Spin(n) -> SO(n) covering map.
    - ORIENTABILITY: existence of a consistent choice of orientation on TM, equivalently det(transition functions) > 0 for all overlaps, equivalently a nowhere-vanishing n-form.

    **Step 2: Framing => Spin structure.**
    If M is framed, the frame bundle F(M) admits a global section s. This global section reduces the structure group from GL(n) to the trivial group {e}. Since {e} lifts trivially to any covering group, the SO(n) bundle (obtained by Gram-Schmidt from the framing + a metric) lifts to Spin(n). More concretely: a framed manifold has trivial tangent bundle, hence all characteristic classes vanish, in particular w_1 = 0 (orientable) and w_2 = 0 (spin). Therefore framing => spin.

    Note: the converse is false. S^2 is spin (w_2 = 0 since H^2(S^2, Z_2) = Z_2 but the obstruction vanishes for dimensional reasons in this case -- actually S^2 is NOT spin since w_2 != 0; S^3 IS spin but not frameable -- wait, S^3 IS parallelizable). Correct example: CP^2 is orientable but not spin (w_2 != 0). S^4 is spin but not frameable (pi_3(SO(4)) obstruction).

    **Step 3: Spin structure => Orientability.**
    A spin structure requires an orientation as part of its definition (we need the SO(n) bundle first, which requires orientation). So spin => oriented (by definition of spin structure). For non-orientable manifolds, one uses pin structures instead.

    **Step 4: Paper 6's lattice as a framing.**
    Paper 6's self-modeling lattice has:
    - Local algebra M_n(C)^sa at each site (providing local degrees of freedom).
    - SWAP Hamiltonian H = sum J * SWAP connecting nearest neighbors.
    - In the continuum limit (L >> a), this produces a smooth manifold with a metric (from the area law / Jacobson argument).

    The lattice structure provides a FRAMING because:
    (a) The lattice itself defines a preferred set of directions at each point (the lattice basis vectors e_1, ..., e_d in a d-dimensional lattice). These are globally defined (by the periodic structure of the lattice).
    (b) In the continuum limit, these lattice directions become a global frame field on the emergent manifold.
    (c) This is STRONGER than a spin structure: it gives a full trivialization, not just a double-cover lift.

    Note the important caveat: the continuum-limit manifold inherits the framing from the lattice, but the physical manifold may only need the spin structure (which is guaranteed by the framing). The lattice framing is a UV artifact that provides more structure than the IR physics requires.

    **Step 5: Time-orientation from the lattice.**
    Paper 6's lattice has:
    - Lieb-Robinson velocity providing emergent causal structure (lattice.tex, Sec. II.C).
    - The Heisenberg time evolution exp(-iHt) defining a preferred time direction.
    - The Hamiltonian H itself distinguishing past from future (the state evolves forward in t).

    This provides a TIME-ORIENTATION in the continuum limit: the direction of Hamiltonian time evolution defines the future-pointing timelike vector field. Combined with the spatial framing from the lattice, the full structure is a time-oriented, space-oriented, framed Lorentzian manifold -- exactly what is needed for Weyl spinors.

    **Step 6: State the result.**
    PROPOSITION (Lattice framing provides spin structure): The self-modeling lattice of Paper 6, in its continuum limit (L >> a), produces an emergent manifold that is:
    (i) Framed (from lattice basis vectors) -- hence w_1 = w_2 = 0.
    (ii) Spin (from framing, since framing => spin).
    (iii) Time-oriented (from Hamiltonian time evolution).
    (iv) Space-oriented (from lattice orientation).
    Therefore all conditions for Weyl spinor bundles (Lawson-Michelsohn) are satisfied.
  </action>
