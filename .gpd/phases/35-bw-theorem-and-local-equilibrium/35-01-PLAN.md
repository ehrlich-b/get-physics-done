    **Part II: Lattice-BW Entanglement Hamiltonian**

    Step 6: Write the lattice-BW ansatz from Giudici et al. (2018). For a half-chain (or half-plane) bipartition with entanglement cut at x_perp = 0, the BW entanglement Hamiltonian is:

      H_ent^{BW}(A) = (2pi / c_s) sum_{x in A} x_perp * h_x     ... (35.1)

    where x_perp is the perpendicular distance of site x from the entanglement cut (in lattice units a=1), c_s = 1.659 Ja is the spin-wave velocity from Phase 33/34, and h_x is the local Hamiltonian density. For the Heisenberg model, h_x = J * S_x . S_{x+1} (the bond energy at site x).

    Step 7: Dimensional analysis of Eq. (35.1). With a = 1:
    - [2pi / c_s] = [1/J] (since c_s has units J*a = J in lattice units)
    - [x_perp] = [dimensionless] (lattice sites)
    - [h_x] = [J] (energy)
    - [H_ent^{BW}] = [1/J] * [1] * [J] = [dimensionless]
    This is correct: the entanglement Hamiltonian is dimensionless (exponent of the density matrix).

    Step 8: Trace the 2pi factor. The continuum BW gives K_A = 2pi K_boost. In the lattice version, the 2pi appears as the prefactor 2pi/c_s in Eq. (35.1). The physical content is: the effective inverse temperature at distance x_perp from the cut is beta(x_perp) = 2pi x_perp / c_s. At x_perp = 1 (first site), beta(1) = 2pi/c_s = 2pi/(1.659 J) = 3.786/J. This is the lattice analog of the Unruh temperature varying with distance from the horizon.

    Step 9: Connect to SRF = 0.9993 from Paper 6 Phase 11. The SRF (Short-Range Fraction) measures what fraction of the Frobenius weight of K_A is in range-1 (nearest-neighbor) Pauli strings. BW predicts K_A is a local operator, dominated by the local Hamiltonian density weighted by distance from the boundary. Therefore:

    BW prediction -> K_A ~ sum x_perp h_x -> K_A is dominated by nearest-neighbor terms (since h_x = J S_x . S_{x+1} is nearest-neighbor) -> SRF should be close to 1.

    The measured SRF = 0.9993 confirms this: 99.93% of K_A weight is in nearest-neighbor terms, exactly as BW predicts. This is the numerical validation of the BW structure.

    Cite Paper 6 Phase 11 and code/modular_hamiltonian_check.py.

    Step 10: State the continuum limit connection. The lattice-BW ansatz Eq. (35.1) approximates the exact K_A with corrections that vanish as a/L -> 0:

      K_A^{exact} = H_ent^{BW} + O((a/L)^2)

    where L is the subsystem size. The O((a/L)^2) scaling is expected from standard lattice-to-continuum corrections (same scaling as the isotropy corrections in Phase 34, LRNZ-01). In the continuum limit, H_ent^{BW} -> 2pi int_A d^d x (x_perp / c_s) T_00(x), which is the CHM modular Hamiltonian for a half-space (Casini-Huerta-Myers 2011).

    **Part III: Type I vs Type III -- Honest Statement**

    Step 11: State the type gap explicitly.

    (a) On the finite lattice: the observable algebra for region A is M_{d_A}(C) -- a type I factor (matrix algebra). The modular operator is Delta = rho_A tensor rho_{A^c}^{-1}, and the modular flow is sigma_t(a) = rho_A^{it} a rho_A^{-it}. This is well-defined but does NOT have the geometric interpretation of a Lorentz boost.

    (b) In the continuum limit (thermodynamic limit + continuum limit): the local algebra R(W_R) for a Rindler wedge is expected to be type III_1 (Haag 1996, Fredenhagen 1985). The BW theorem applies in this setting, giving sigma_t = Lambda(-2pi t).

    (c) The bridge: the lattice-BW ansatz is the lattice fingerprint of the continuum BW structure. It is an approximation that becomes exact in the double limit (thermodynamic + continuum). On any finite lattice, it is approximate, with accuracy measured by the SRF and the Frobenius norm comparison.
