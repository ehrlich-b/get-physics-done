  <action>
    Extend the first-order condition analysis to general D in the full n^2(n^2+1)-dim moduli space.

    ANALYTICAL part (append to derivations/09-first-order-condition.md):

    Step 1: Structure of general D on the doubled space.
    General D = [[0, M^dag], [M, 0]] where M: H_+ -> H_- satisfies M = J_+ M^T J_+.
    Under Barrett iso, M acts as an operator on M_n(C). In the gamma-eigenspace basis (Sym_p, Skew_ap -> Skew_p, Sym_ap), M has sub-blocks:
    M = [[M_{11}, M_{12}], [M_{21}, -M_{11}^T]] with M_{12} = M_{12}^T, M_{21} = M_{21}^T.

    Step 2: Compute [D, pi(a)] for general D.
    On the doubled space, D(X_p, X_ap) = (M^dag(X_ap), M(X_p)) (schematically -- M maps between gamma eigenspaces).
    Under Barrett iso, the action of M on the single sector M_n(C) is NOT necessarily L_K + R_K.
    For general M, the map X -> M(X) involves BOTH left and right multiplication and potentially more complex operations.
    Compute [D, pi(a)] = D pi(a) - pi(a) D component by component.

    Step 3: Analyze when [D, pi(a)] has R-type components.
    For Barrett-form D: M(X) = KX + XK, so [M, L_a](X) = [K,a]X = L_{[K,a]}(X) -- pure left.
    For general M: [M, L_a] may have R components if M contains R-type (right multiplication) terms.
    This is the structural reason why general D might give non-trivial first-order condition.

    NUMERICAL part (extend tests/test_first_order.py):

    Step 4: Build constraint matrix for general D at n=2.
    Use the moduli space basis from test_dirac_moduli.py (the 20-dimensional basis at n=2).
    For a general D (linear combination of basis elements with random coefficients):
      For each candidate a parameterized by n^2 complex components (vectorized as 2n^2 real components):
        For each basis b = E_{kl}:
          Compute [[D, L_a], R_b] as a 2n^2 x 2n^2 matrix.
          Vectorize as rows of the constraint matrix.
    Compute null space of constraint matrix via SVD.
    dim(null space) = dim(A_F).

    Step 5: Sample 20 random D from the full moduli space at n=2.
    For each: compute dim(A_F). Record results.
    Also sample 10 D from the Barrett subspace (dim 3 at n=2). Verify dim(A_F) = 4 for all.
    Compare Barrett vs non-Barrett dim(A_F) distributions.

    Step 6: Repeat at n=3 (dim 90, A_F target 9) and n=4 (dim 272, A_F target 16).
    At n=3,4: sample 10 random D from full moduli and 5 from Barrett subspace.

    Step 7: If A_F is a proper subalgebra for some D:
    - Identify dim(A_F) and check if it corresponds to a known algebra.
    - Check *-subalgebra closure.
    - Compare to dim(C + H + M_3(C)) = 14 at n=4.
    - Determine if A_F is a direct sum of matrix algebras (Wedderburn-Artin).

    If A_F = M_n(C) for ALL D tested:
    - Document that the bimodule structure H = 2 x C^{n^2} makes the first-order condition trivial for ALL D in the moduli space.
    - This means the self-modeling M_n(C) construction needs additional structure (direct sum decomposition) to produce the SM algebra.
